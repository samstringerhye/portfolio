# CVS Shop Website Redesign

When the system is right, everything downstream gets better. CVS.com is one of the largest pharmacy retailer e-commerce platforms in the US, serving customers who might be filling a prescription, buying skincare, or both in the same session. The shop had accumulated years of inconsistency — unclear hierarchy, fractured navigation, and a visual language that had drifted from the brand. The modernization was about restoring clarity, not reinventing the wheel.

</ProseGroup>

<ProseGroup>

## The Problem

CVS sits at an unusual intersection — pharmacy and general retailer — which means the platform serves a wider range of intent than most e-commerce sites. Over time, the shop grew through acquisitions rather than deliberate system thinking, producing fractured type scales, inconsistent icons, and components built independently rather than from shared parts. And because CVS.com is a live platform with real customer volume, fixing it required a disciplined approach to foundations rather than a greenfield rebuild.

</ProseGroup>

<ProseGroup>

## The Work

I worked from the ground up — typographic framework first, then a grid-based icon system using CVS brand red, then a component library covering homepage modules, product pages, and category navigation. Starting with typography meant every downstream component inherited consistency rather than having to engineer it in later, and scoping everything to desktop and mobile from the start prevented the usual pattern of adapting after the fact.

</ProseGroup>

## What I Learned

Foundation work is where the most leverage is. When the typographic scale is resolved, product teams stop making ad hoc sizing decisions; when the icon system is consistent, no one debates stroke weight. What the system unlocked was speed downstream — consistent inputs produce consistent outputs, freeing design and engineering capacity for harder problems like checkout and pharmacy flows.
