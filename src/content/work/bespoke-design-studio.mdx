# Samsung Bespoke Design Studio

Making a complex configurator feel simple through sequencing. Samsung's Bespoke line lets customers configure appliances down to the panel finish, but the online experience wasn't matching the showroom. I owned visual design for a tool that had to guide users through dozens of decisions without overwhelming them.

</ProseGroup>

## The Problem

A customer configuring a Bespoke fridge has to pick a model, then a size, then colours for four separate door panels. Show all of that at once and people bail. The early prototypes confirmed this. Show too little and they don't understand their options. The core challenge was progressive disclosure: finding the right order so each step felt obvious. In-store, a salesperson sequences those choices naturally. Online, the interface has to do that work.

</ProseGroup>

## The Work

I owned visual design, not strategy or UX research, but the surface layer where those decisions become real to a user. The key move was a stepped progress indicator showing users how many decisions remained. That cut mid-flow abandonment: users could see they were three steps from done rather than wondering if the choices would ever end.

</ProseGroup>

## Wireframes

Multiple rounds of wireframing explored how to sequence the configuration steps. Each decision depended on the previous one (you can't pick panel colours until you've chosen a model), but users needed to see the full scope before committing to step one.

</ProseGroup>

  ## User Flow

  The user flow mapped every decision point and branch. The stepped progress indicator, visible at every stage, gave users a mental model of the whole journey before they started. That's what made the configurator feel manageable rather than endless.

  </ProseGroup>

  ## Design Execution

  I led UI and interaction design across mobile and desktop. Motion reinforced actions, educational touchpoints reduced hesitation, and the system was built to grow with the platform. Every visual decision served the sequencing: colour swatches appeared only when relevant, model comparisons showed only meaningful differences.

## What I Learned

The hardest part was learning when to stop designing options. Every customization path increased cognitive load, and the configurator was already asking users to make visual decisions about appliances they'd never seen in person. The real discipline was editing: cutting choices that didn't earn their complexity, trusting that fewer, better-framed options would convert higher than an exhaustive catalog.

</ProseGroup>
