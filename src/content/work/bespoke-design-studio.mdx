# Samsung Bespoke Design Studio

Making a complex configurator feel simple through sequencing. Samsung's Bespoke line lets customers configure appliances down to the panel finish, but the online experience wasn't delivering the same sense of control as the showroom floor. I owned visual design for a tool that had to guide users through dozens of decisions without overwhelming them.

</ProseGroup>

## The Problem

A customer configuring a Bespoke fridge has to pick a model, then a size, then colours for four separate door panels. Show all of that at once and people bail — the early prototypes confirmed this. Show too little and they don't understand their options. The core challenge was progressive disclosure: finding the right order to present decisions so each step felt obvious, not overwhelming. In-store, a salesperson sequences those choices naturally. Online, the interface has to do that work.

</ProseGroup>

## The Work

I owned visual design — not strategy or UX research, but the surface layer where those decisions become real to a user. The key design decision was a stepped progress indicator that showed users the scope of remaining decisions upfront. That single addition cut abandonment in the configurator's mid-flow, because users could see they were three steps from done rather than wondering if the choices would ever end.

</ProseGroup>

### Wireframes

Multiple rounds of wireframing explored how to sequence the configuration steps. The core tension: each decision depended on the previous one (you can't pick panel colours until you've chosen a model), but users needed to understand the full scope before committing to step one.

</ProseGroup>

## What I Learned

The hardest part of this project was sequencing. Progressive disclosure sounds straightforward in theory, but in practice it's a series of trade-offs — every decision you defer to a later step is a decision the user might not reach. The stepped progress indicator worked because it gave users a mental model of the whole journey before they started. That's what made the configurator feel manageable rather than endless.

</ProseGroup>
